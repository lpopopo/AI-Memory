## Image-Level Learning

The 16 carousel images make the framework clearer than the title-only public checker:

- The author explicitly says this is not prediction, homework copying, stock recommendation, or investment advice. Companies are examples for industry-chain discussion.
- Main framing: after a 2026 H1 "collapse-style upward move", AI's dark line is shifting from simply funding capacity to proving cash flow and delivery. The market ruler is moving from "how much CapEx burned" to "show me the money".
- The AI chain is compared to an endless Double 11 logistics system: commands become packages that need processing, inspection, sorting, and delivery.
- Transmission sequence:
  - GPU / compute: `NVDA`, `AMD`.
  - Memory / storage: HBM, NAND / storage examples such as `MU`, `SNDK`.
  - Larger clusters need optical / interconnect: `MRVL`, `AVGO`, `MXL`, `AXTI`, `LITE`, `COHR`, `AAOI`.
  - More applications require CPU scheduling: `INTC`, `AMD`.
  - Capacity bottleneck leads back to fabs / tools: `ASML`, `AMAT`, `KLA`.
- The article separates three waves:
  - First wave: build warehouses, buy fleets, rent compute capacity; examples include `CRWV`, `NBIS`, `SNDK`, `NVDA`, `MU`, and new-cloud / neocloud platforms.
  - Second wave: build sorters, shelves, inspection, and "labeling" equipment. The author says the next scarcity may be validation / inspection rather than fabrication alone.
  - Third wave: connect all warehouses into a trunk sorting network; the author frames interconnect as the durable bottleneck.
- On the first wave: `CRWV` backlog was cited as rising from about USD 25.9B to USD 99.4B in about a year, but the author warns the issue is not orders, it is that matching the distribution network costs a lot of money. Existing winners can become heavier because expansion pulls capex, depreciation, interest, and working capital.
- On the second wave: as HBM stacks move from 8 layers to 16 / 24 layers, more insertion / system-level testing is required. If one layer or base die fails, the whole package can be wasted. The author highlights testing / validation as a scarce step and names `TER` and `KLA` as representative automated test / inspection equipment companies.
- On the third wave: interconnect is treated as the author's most important and durable layer. Once packages are processed, they still must be sorted and sent through a trunk network. The author connects this to prior MRVL / ALAB / optical module work.
- The author states the strongest bottleneck may be interconnect: when transfer becomes fast enough, HBM can be pooled to improve memory utilization and unlock compute.
- `CRDO` appears as a more direct AEC / copper-cable transmission beneficiary: management targets FY26 revenue around USD 1.34B, roughly tripling, with non-GAAP gross margin around 68% in the author's cited framework.
- The author compares short-reach AEC copper and optical modules: under about 3 meters copper can be cheap and easy; beyond about 7 meters copper becomes insufficient and optical is needed. Each AI sorter / server platform needs transmission equipment around it.
- The author emphasizes that interconnect may be less sensitive to which platform wins. Whether the warehouse order goes to Nvidia, AWS Trainium, or Google TPU, packages still need this sorting / delivery network.
- Signals to track:
  - Testing layer: whether HBM layer counts rise and whether insertion / system-level test penetration keeps increasing.
  - Interconnect layer: whether content per sorter / server continues to rise and optical products take a larger share of incremental volume.
  - Custom silicon layer: whether MRVL custom silicon share can rise from about 20% in FY27 toward doubling in FY28.
